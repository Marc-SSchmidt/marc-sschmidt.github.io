Toxicological Hazards from Air Pollutants Following Destruction of Oil Depots in Tehran and Karaj

Release of Toxic Hydrocarbons, Sulfur and Nitrogen Oxides: Inhalation Risks, Protective Measures, and the Potential for Acid Rain Formation in Urban Areas

2026-03-11

The strikes on oil depots in Tehran and Karaj ignited large-scale fires that released incompletely combusted hydrocarbons, soot particles, and a range of highly toxic compounds into the urban air. What is visually apparent as a black smoke cloud constitutes, from a toxicological standpoint, a complex mixture whose hazard potential extends far beyond the immediate fire zone. This article analyzes which pollutants are generated during the combustion of crude oil and petroleum products, the pathways through which they enter the human body, and the acute and chronic health risks to be expected for the affected population. Particular attention is given to population groups with heightened vulnerability — including children, the elderly, and individuals with pre-existing respiratory or cardiovascular conditions.

Media Review: What the Reports Document

International media coverage of the consequences of the strikes on the oil depots in Tehran and Karaj began immediately and focused on three core aspects: the composition of the released pollutants, official warnings, and the symptoms experienced by the population.

Den Ausgangspunkt der offiziellen Kommunikation bildete die Iranische Rothalbmond-Gesellschaft. Sie warnte öffentlich vor der Freisetzung „bedeutender Mengen toxischer Kohlenwasserstoffe, Schwefel- und Stickoxyde” [1] – eine Formulierung, die in nahezu identischer Form von zahlreichen Medien aufgegriffen wurde [2] [3]. Parallel dazu erklärte Irans Umweltbehörde, gefährdete Gruppen – Kinder, Ältere, Herz- und Lungenkranke sowie Schwangere – sollten den Aufenthalt im Freien strikt meiden [4].

Besondere Aufmerksamkeit galt dem gleichzeitigen Auftreten von Regen und Schadstoffwolke. Der Rote Halbmond bezeichnete den Niederschlag als „hochgradig sauer und gefährlich” und warnte vor chemischen Verätzungen der Haut sowie schweren Lungenschäden [5]. Auch die Junge Welt griff diesen Aspekt auf und nannte den Niederschlag explizit „Ölregen”: Rückstände aus den brennenden Treibstoffdepots, die sich mit Wassertropfen vermischt hatten und die toxischen Stoffe in Oberflächengewässer und Böden einzutragen drohten [6].

Merkur added that residents reported the city smelled of carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, and aromatic hydrocarbons — including benzene, naphthalene, and xylene, all compounds with carcinogenic potential upon prolonged exposure [7]. Black streaks of oil and dust on building facades, soot-covered balconies, and oil-contaminated puddles in the streets documented the spatial extent of the contamination, which stretched dozens of kilometers from the fire's epicenter [1] [3].

Several reports quoted Tehran Province emergency coordinator Saeed Mehrsoroush with a nuanced assessment: the debate about acid rain dominating social media was distracting from the more acute problem — direct inhalation of gases remaining in the air. He explicitly recommended wearing N95 or FFP2 respirators and clarified that simple medical masks provided insufficient protection, as toxic vapors pass through them readily [8].

The Guardian described the situation through eyewitness accounts that painted a vivid picture of the acute exposure: burning eyes, sore throats, and respiratory problems dominated the descriptions [9]. Time quoted a female resident who, after just 15 minutes in a car, reported headaches, sore lips, and a burning sensation she compared to diluted tear gas [5].

Die WHO meldete sich ebenfalls zu Wort. Laut Jerusalem Post bestätigte die Organisation, dass der schwarze und saure Regen eine reale Gefahr für die Atemwege darstelle, und unterstützte die Empfehlung, Innenräume aufzusuchen [10]. Gleichzeitig wurde betont, das Risiko sinke, sofern keine erneuten Angriffe auf Treibstoffanlagen erfolgten – andernfalls sei die Lage erneut „problematisch” [10].

SRF quoted Gabriel da Silva, a combustion chemist at the University of Melbourne, with an assessment of short- and long-term health consequences: in the short term, headaches and respiratory problems were to be expected, especially in those with pre-existing conditions such as asthma; in the long term, ultrafine particles entering the bloodstream via the lungs could cause cancer as well as neurological and cardiovascular diseases [11]. As a historical reference point, the report cited the burning oil fields of Kuwait during the 1991 Gulf War, where subsequent epidemiological studies documented a threefold increase in cancer incidence along with elevated rates of neurological disorders, asthma, and allergies [11].

Coverage of environmental consequences beyond the immediate health hazard remained sparse. Only SRF addressed the ecological dimension in depth: acid rain could damage aquatic ecosystems, prevent fish eggs from hatching, and leach nutrients from soils [11]. The WHO added that damaged petroleum facilities threatened food and water supplies in addition to air quality [6].

Contextual background was provided by Al Jazeera in a report from November 2025 — before the strikes: Tehran and numerous other Iranian cities were already suffering from chronically poor air quality, partly due to the combustion of mazut — a sulfur-rich petroleum residue — in power plants. The deputy health minister had reported nearly 59,000 deaths in the preceding year from PM2.5 particles alone (respirable fine particles with a diameter below 2.5 micrometers) [12]. The strikes on the oil depots thus struck a population whose airways were already compromised by years of structural air pollution.

Meteorological factors further exacerbated the situation. The head of the Iranian Meteorological Organization's forecast center explained that calm wind conditions were allowing the smoke to linger over the city; further precipitation had been forecast for Sunday and Monday [4]. Tehran's air quality authority anticipated only a gradual improvement once the atmospheric boundary layer rose higher — and the fires were fully extinguished [7].

Toxicological Assessment

Well-Documented Findings

Die Berichterstattung trifft in ihren Kernaussagen zur Schadstoffzusammensetzung überwiegend zu, bedarf aber wissenschaftlicher Schärfung. Die Warnung der Iranischen Rothalbmond-Gesellschaft vor „toxischen Kohlenwasserstoffen, Schwefel- und Stickoxiden” [1] [2] benennt zwar die richtigen Substanzklassen, unterschätzt aber die Komplexität des tatsächlichen Emissionsgemischs.

Dass brennende Mineralöllager Schwefeldioxid (SO₂) in erheblichen Mengen freisetzen, ist gut belegt. SO₂ entsteht bei der Verbrennung schwefelreicher Kraftstoffe – Heizöl, Rohöl und insbesondere Mazut – durch Oxidation des im Molekül gebundenen Schwefels. Die Atemwegsreizung durch SO₂ setzt bereits bei Konzentrationen von weniger als zwei Parts per Million (ppm; Millionstel Volumenanteile) ein, also weit unterhalb der akut lebensbedrohlichen Schwelle [13]. Stickstoffoxide bilden sich zusätzlich bei den hohen Verbrennungstemperaturen aus dem Luftstickstoff – ein Prozess, der in der References als thermisches NO₍x₎ bezeichnet wird. Beide Substanzklassen wirken als Vorläufer für die Säurekomponenten des sauren Regens: SO₂ reagiert mit atmosphärischer Feuchte zu schwefliger Säure und Schwefelsäure, NO₂ zu Salpetersäure [14].

The characterization of aromatic hydrocarbons — benzene, xylene, naphthalene — by Merkur [7] is scientifically accurate and is supported by measurement data from the reference event of the burning Kuwait oil fields in 1991. Etzel and Ashley documented significantly elevated blood concentrations of ethylbenzene, benzene, xylene, styrene, and toluene in US firefighters deployed there compared to the general population [15]. Benzene is the only one of these solvents classified by the IARC in the highest carcinogenicity category [16]; its high volatility explains why it escapes in substantial quantities during a depot fire both through direct evaporation from unignited product stocks and through incomplete combustion.

That ultrafine particles can enter the bloodstream via the lungs and cause cardiovascular damage there, as quoted combustion chemist Gabriel da Silva explained to SRF [11], is consistent with the current state of research. Multiple studies have shown that chemical constituents of particulate matter — especially from combustion sources — are associated with cardiovascular hospitalizations and increased mortality [17]. The polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH; compounds with multiple condensed aromatic rings formed during incomplete combustion) adsorbed onto soot particles are considered genotoxic; Kelsey and colleagues demonstrated that air particles from the vicinity of the Kuwait oil fires dose-dependently induced sister chromatid exchanges and mutations in human lymphocytes [18]. The systematic role of PAH in combustion emissions as mutagenic and carcinogenic agents is extensively documented [19].

Die berichteten Akutsymptome der Bevölkerung – Augenbrennen, Halsschmerzen, Atemprobleme, Kopfschmerzen – sind in ihrer Kombination toxikologisch plausibel und entsprechen dem bekannten Reizprofil von SO₂, NO₂ und aromatischen Kohlenwasserstoffen [20]. Der Vergleich einer Bewohnerin mit „verdünntem Tränengas” [5] ist phänomenologisch nachvollziehbar: Akrolein und Formaldehyd, beides typische Nebenprodukte der Ölverbrennung, weisen ein ähnliches Reizprofil auf wie die in Tränengas enthaltenen Reizstoffe. Der Hinweis des Teheraner Notfallbeauftragten, dass N95- oder FFP2-Masken erforderlich seien und medizinische Einmalmasken gegenüber toxischen Dämpfen keinen Schutz böten [8], ist korrekt: Partikelfiltermasken sind nicht für gasförmige Schadstoffe ausgelegt; gegen Dämpfe wären Aktivkohlefilter notwendig, die im Alltag nicht verfügbar sind.

Die von SRF angeführten Langzeitfolgen der Kuwaiter Ölbrände – erhöhte Krebsinzidenz, gesteigerte Raten von Asthma, COPD und neurologischen Erkrankungen [11] – sind in der epidemiologischen References grundsätzlich belegt. Australische Golfkriegsveteranen zeigten im Vergleich zu einer militärischen Kontrollgruppe signifikant erhöhte Prävalenzen von Asthma und erstmals nach dem Krieg diagnostizierter Bronchitis, ohne dass allerdings messbar schlechtere Lungenfunktionswerte gefunden wurden – was auf Symptombelastung bei noch kompensierter Funktion hindeutet [21]. US-Armeeangehörige berichteten während ihres Kuwait-Einsatzes gehäuft über Augen- und Atemwegsreizungen, Kurzatmigkeit, Husten und Ausschläge, wobei die Symptomhäufigkeit mit der berichteten Nähe zu den Feuern korrelierte [22].

Quantitative Assessment

The fundamental deficiency of all the reporting is the complete absence of measured pollutant concentrations. All qualitative warnings — whether from the Red Crescent Society [1], the WHO [10], or the provincial emergency coordinator [8] — cannot be toxicologically classified without measurement data. This is not a criticism of the reporting, but the description of a fundamental information gap.

Nevertheless, plausibility frameworks can be outlined. The WHO guideline for the daily mean of PM₂.₅ is less than one-fortieth of the typical particulate concentrations measured in the immediate vicinity of major industrial fires [13]. The modeling study of the Buncefield fire showed that under unfavorable meteorological conditions, even densely populated urban areas could face tens of thousands of people in health-relevant exposure — and Buncefield was an event of considerably smaller scale than the Tehran depots [23]. The cases actually documented in the emergency department register after Buncefield — predominantly respiratory symptoms, no fatalities, but a significant proportion requiring referral — illustrate the clinical spectrum to be expected even from a temporally limited event under well-monitored conditions [24].

The carbon monoxide scenario deserves particular attention. Reports quoting residents speaking of a smell of CO [7] are, strictly speaking, physically inaccurate — CO is colorless and odorless. The described smell derives from accompanying volatile organic compounds. This does not mean, however, that CO was absent: on the contrary, incomplete combustion of large quantities of oil is expected to produce CO in substantial concentrations. The WHO short-term reference values for CO are certainly exceeded during exposure in an extensive fire plume; headaches — described by numerous residents — occur from a carboxyhemoglobin fraction of around ten percent [25]. The testimony of the resident who reported headaches after only fifteen minutes in a vehicle [5] is consistent with this exposure.

For benzene: since there is no toxicologically safe level of exposure — the IARC classifies it as a Group 1 carcinogen [16] — any increase above background levels is epidemiologically relevant. As measurement data from Kuwait showed, ethylbenzene concentrations in the blood of firefighters were approximately tenfold above the US population reference range [15]. Benzene levels were also significantly elevated, though less extremely so — a finding indicative of acute rather than chronic exposure.

For the acid rain, the decisive parameter is missing: a measured pH value. From the Niger Delta, where petroleum exploration and combustion represent the primary sources of atmospheric acidification, pH values below 4.5 have been documented in precipitation samples [26] [27]. Whether the Tehran precipitation reached or exceeded this magnitude cannot be answered without measurement. At pH values below 3 — physically plausible for major industrial fires of this scale — direct corrosive effects on skin and mucous membranes would be medically relevant.

The baseline figure cited by Al Jazeera of nearly 59,000 annual PM₂.₅-related deaths in Iran [12] contextualizes the event: Tehran's population was already carrying an exceptionally high chronic pollutant burden before the fires. This pre-existing burden is clinically significant because it reduces the physiological reserve of the airways and increases vulnerability to acute exposure peaks. The combustion of mazut in Tehran's power plants, already identified before the attack as the primary source of background SO₂ pollution [12], provided an unfavorable baseline upon which the fire event was superimposed.

Underreported and Underestimated Risks

The latency problem of NO₂ is entirely absent from the reporting. Coverage focuses almost exclusively on immediately perceptible irritation effects — burning eyes, sore throat, immediate shortness of breath. Yet the most medically serious acute risk of nitrogen dioxide goes unmentioned: toxic pulmonary edema (alveolar edema; fluid accumulation in the lung alveoli with consequent oxygen deficiency). NO₂ is considerably less water-soluble than SO₂ and therefore penetrates deeper into the respiratory tract without being filtered out in the upper airways. Particularly insidious is the typical course: exposure is followed by a subjective sense of wellbeing that can last four to twenty-four hours before a potentially life-threatening edema develops. Individuals who believe themselves safe after temporary symptom relief and forgo medical monitoring thereby carry an underestimated risk.

The PAH burden on soot particles is structurally underreported. Benzene, naphthalene, and xylene found their way into the coverage [7]; the low-volatility PAH adsorbed onto soot particles and transported deep into the lungs, however, go unmentioned. These compounds are considered the most carcinogenically relevant fraction of oil combustion emissions in the long term. Benzo[a]pyrene as the benchmark substance has been classified as a human carcinogen by the IARC [28]; the entire group of PAH in outdoor air combustion emissions likewise [29]. Srogi has comprehensively described the environmental monitoring data situation for PAH, highlighting the persistence of these compounds and their adsorption onto particulate matter [30]. Since PAH exposure occurs via inhalation and dermal routes — with the latter additionally relevant during oil rain — the overall exposure scenario is more complex than depicted in the reporting.

The fetotoxic dimension of CO and PAH remains invisible. Pregnant women were identified as a risk group by Iran's Environmental Protection Organization [4], but the biological rationale is missing. Carbon monoxide crosses the placenta; fetal hemoglobin has a higher CO-binding affinity than maternal hemoglobin, meaning that fetal carboxyhemoglobin rises higher and falls more slowly at equivalent exposure than the mother's [25]. PAH metabolites are placenta-permeable and have fetotoxic potential. The fact that a pregnant woman in Tehran's population bears a different risk profile from a non-pregnant adult during a multi-hour exposure event is not explained in the reporting.

Die dermale Expositionsroute via „Ölregen” ist unterschätzt. Die Berichterstattung thematisiert den sauren Regen überwiegend als Inhalationsrisiko oder unter dem Gesichtspunkt der Ätzwirkung auf Haut [5] [6]. Wenig Aufmerksamkeit erhält, dass Ölrückstände auf der Haut als Resorptionsvehikel für lipophile Schadstoffe wirken: Die fettlösende Wirkung der Kohlenwasserstoffe stört die Hautbarriere und kann die perkutane Aufnahme von Benzol und PAK erhöhen – eine Expositionsroute, die bei kurzen Aufenthalten im Freien relevant werden kann und sich der unmittelbaren Symptomwahrnehmung entzieht.

The hospital surveillance vacuum is a structural deficiency. From comparable events we know that refinery and depot fires lead to measurable increases in emergency department presentations [31]. The Buncefield fire, despite considerably lower urban population density and shorter burn duration, resulted in over 240 emergency department visits, a substantial proportion of which involved respiratory symptoms requiring medical follow-up [24]. No comparable surveillance data are available for Tehran. Without systematically collected clinical data, neither a timely assessment of the extent of harm nor subsequent epidemiological follow-up is possible.

The long-term cancer risk from occupational exposure to combustion products is underestimated. Firefighters, rescue personnel, and emergency responders who worked in direct proximity to the fires for hours or days carry a substantially higher exposure than the general population. That occupational exposure to oil mists and combustion products is associated with elevated risks for meningiomas (brain membrane tumors) — with an odds ratio of 1.57 for ever-exposed compared to never-exposed individuals — is shown by the INTEROCC study [32]. Childhood cancers show an association with proximity to atmospheric emission hotspots from oil combustion sources in British study data [33]. These long-term, latent risks are naturally absent from acute reporting — but should be considered in health follow-up care.

Meteorological follow-on scenarios have not been modeled. The reference to calm wind conditions and expected further precipitation [4] is documented in the reporting but not elaborated in its consequences. Atmospheric dispersion modeling — carried out retrospectively for Buncefield under variable meteorological scenarios — showed that unfavorable wind directions could have multiplied the number of exposed individuals and resulting health effects compared to the scenario that actually occurred [23]. For Tehran, a metropolis with exponentially higher population density, such modeling would have been urgently required — and was evidently not available.

Conclusion

The strikes on the oil depots in Tehran and Karaj released a complex mixture of airborne pollutants whose toxicological profile significantly exceeds the publicly communicated warnings. The official statements — sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, hydrocarbons — correctly identify the relevant substance classes, but capture neither the polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons nor the particulate burden from soot in their full health significance. What the population in the affected districts inhaled was not a single substance, but a reactive multi-component mixture with additive and potentially synergistic effects.

The symptoms documented in the media — respiratory irritation, headaches, burning eyes — are toxicologically plausible and consistent with the known effect profiles of the released substances. At the same time, these acute symptoms represent only the visible portion of the damage. Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons such as benzo[a]pyrene are genotoxic and carcinogenic; their effects manifest not in hours, but in years. Particularly vulnerable groups — children, the elderly, pregnant women, and those with pre-existing respiratory or cardiac conditions — bear a disproportionately high risk.

The official recommendation to avoid outdoor exposure was appropriate given the circumstances. What must be assessed critically, however, is the absence of transparent measurement data: without reliable ambient air measurements, neither acute exposure estimates can be made nor epidemiological follow-up studies meaningfully designed. The reference by several media outlets to rain that fell simultaneously is toxicologically relevant in this context — acid rain can both wash pollutants out of the air and introduce them in water-soluble form into soils and surface waters, thereby opening exposure pathways via drinking water and food.

The most important long-term perspective lies in systematic follow-up care. Major fires at petrochemical facilities are not a singular peak burden whose consequences end when the flames are extinguished. Contaminated soils, polluted surface waters, and the deposition of soot particles in the lungs of exposed individuals are persistent sources of harm. A credible health assessment of the event requires what is so far absent: independent environmental measurements, transparent data publication, and the establishment of a health registry for the most severely affected population.
